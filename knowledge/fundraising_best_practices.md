# Fundraising Best Practices: Practitioner's Reference

A cheat sheet for small nonprofit development teams. Designed for quick reference, not comprehensive study.

---

## Donor Lifecycle and Segmentation

### Pipeline Stages

Every donor moves through a sequence of relationship stages. Knowing where someone sits in the pipeline determines what action to take next.

| Stage | Definition | Typical Action |
|---|---|---|
| Prospect | Has capacity and some affinity; no gift yet | Research, warm introduction |
| First-time donor | Made one gift | Immediate acknowledgment, cultivation |
| Repeat donor | Given 2+ times | Stewardship, gradual upgrade asks |
| Major donor | Gives at a threshold your org defines (e.g., $1,000+) | Personal relationship management |
| Lapsed donor | No gift in 18-36+ months (depends on org) | Re-engagement campaign |

### RFM Analysis

RFM scores donors on three dimensions: **Recency** (how recently did they give?), **Frequency** (how often do they give?), and **Monetary value** (how much do they give?).

For small nonprofits where most donors give once per year, frequency is a weak signal. Adapt RFM by weighting recency and monetary value more heavily:

- **High priority:** Recent gift + high amount (even if only once)
- **Re-engagement target:** High historical amount + not recent
- **Upgrade candidate:** Consistent giver + modest amounts (growing relationship)
- **Low priority:** Small gift, no recency, no frequency pattern

RFM is a ranking tool, not a prediction. Use it to sort your list, not to make final decisions.

### Retention vs. Acquisition

Retaining an existing donor is consistently cheaper than acquiring a new one. Industry estimates place the cost of acquiring a new donor at **5-10x** the cost of retaining an existing one. Retention should almost always be a higher operational priority than acquisition.

The practical implication: before launching a new-donor campaign, confirm that your stewardship of existing donors is strong. A donor who lapses because they felt ignored is a double loss — you lose the future gift and you've spent acquisition dollars that no longer compound.

**Sector benchmark:** Nonprofit donor retention averages roughly 40-45% per year for smaller organizations. Above 60% is considered strong. Below 30% signals a stewardship problem.

### Moves Management

Moves management is the discipline of systematically advancing a donor through cultivation stages. Each "move" is a deliberate action designed to deepen the relationship.

Sequence: **Identification → Qualification → Cultivation → Solicitation → Stewardship**

- **Identification:** Who belongs in the pipeline? (subscriber lists, event attendees, board referrals)
- **Qualification:** Do they have capacity, affinity, and propensity? (see CAP framework below)
- **Cultivation:** Build the relationship before asking. Invitations to events, personal updates, handwritten notes.
- **Solicitation:** The ask. Should feel natural if cultivation was done well.
- **Stewardship:** Thank, report back, keep them engaged. This is cultivation for the next gift.

A common mistake: jumping from identification directly to solicitation. Without cultivation, response rates drop sharply and the relationship is harder to rebuild.

## Lapsed Donor Re-Engagement

### Industry Benchmarks

Typical re-activation rates for lapsed donors run **5-15%**, with rates varying by how long the lapse has been, the quality of the prior relationship, and the channel used. Personal outreach (phone call from a known relationship) consistently outperforms email or mail.

### Signals a Lapsed Donor May Be Ready

Watch for behavioral signals that suggest renewed interest:

- Renewed or active subscription to your publication
- Attendance at an event (in-person or virtual)
- Recent email opens or clicks, especially on content-driven sends (not just appeals)
- Engagement with your social media content
- Life event: new job, retirement, departure from another institutional affiliation

Any of these signals is a prompt to escalate personal outreach.

### Best Practices for Re-Engagement

- **Acknowledge the gap explicitly.** A message that pretends nothing happened feels off. A brief, warm acknowledgment ("We've missed you") performs better than an appeal that ignores the lapse.
- **Start with a soft ask.** Reintroduce the relationship before asking for money. A cultivation touch (newsletter, event invitation, personal note) before the ask improves conversion.
- **Graduated ask amounts.** Begin the re-engagement ask at roughly **50% of the donor's last gift**. A lower ask is easier to say yes to and reestablishes the giving habit. Upgrade can come in subsequent cycles.
- **Timing:** The optimal window for a re-engagement campaign is **18-24 months** after the last gift. Before 18 months, the donor may not feel lapsed; after 36 months, the relationship cost rises significantly.
- **Channel priority:** Personal phone call > personalized email > direct mail. Mass email alone has low re-activation rates for lapsed major donors.

### Win-Back Campaign Structure

A practical win-back sequence:

1. **Touch 1 (month 1):** Cultivation touch — share impact, no ask. Personal if possible.
2. **Touch 2 (month 2-3):** Soft ask with acknowledgment of lapse and graduated amount.
3. **Touch 3 (month 5-6):** Final appeal, slightly more direct. If no response, move to lower-frequency maintenance list.

Two to three touches over 4-6 months is typical. More than three touches without response has diminishing returns.

## Major Gift Fundraising

### The Donor Pyramid

Visualize your donor base as a pyramid: many small donors at the base, progressively fewer donors at each level, with a small number of major donors at the top. The defining characteristic: **roughly 80% of total dollars typically come from the top 20% of donors** (often called the 80/20 rule, though real distributions vary).

Implication: even a small number of major gift relationships can represent a disproportionate share of revenue. These relationships deserve proportionally more time.

### The CAP Framework for Qualification

Before investing significant cultivation time in a prospect, assess three dimensions:

- **Capacity:** Can they give at a meaningful level? (wealth indicators, known philanthropic history)
- **Affinity:** Do they care about your organization's mission? (subscriber, event attendee, intellectual alignment)
- **Propensity:** Have they shown a pattern of charitable giving? (gifts to peer organizations, board service elsewhere)

A prospect strong on all three is a priority. High capacity without affinity is a common false positive — someone wealthy who has no connection to your mission is unlikely to give, regardless of their net worth.

### Personal Meetings Are Essential

Major gifts rarely happen without face-to-face cultivation. Email and letters maintain relationships; they rarely build them to the point of a major ask. If a donor is at the qualification or cultivation stage, the goal of any written communication is to secure a meeting — not to ask for money.

A reasonable planning estimate: **4-6 major prospect meetings** are needed to close one major gift. This ratio varies widely but sets realistic expectations for a fundraising trip.

### Trip Planning: Center-Out Approach

When planning a fundraising trip to a city, prioritize meetings using the "center-out" model:

1. **Start with your strongest relationships:** Long-term major donors, board members and their connections, anyone who has made a gift in the past 12 months. These meetings are stewardship — they also generate referrals and warm introductions.
2. **Expand to warm prospects:** Subscribers who have attended events, lapsed donors who are showing re-engagement signals, wealth-screened prospects with known affinity.
3. **Do not chase cold prospects** unless a warm introduction is in place. A cold call to a wealthy prospect with no connection to the organization rarely produces a meeting and almost never produces a gift.

Prioritize the meeting list by a combination of: giving history, engagement signals (email, events), relationship depth, and capacity. All four factors matter; capacity alone is not sufficient.

### Ask Ratios

Expect to have meaningful cultivation conversations with **4-6 qualified prospects** for every major gift you close. This is not a failure rate — it is the nature of major gift work. It argues for maintaining a pipeline that is 4-6x larger than your current-year goal in number of relationships.

## Prospect Research and Wealth Screening

### What Wealth Indicators Mean

Wealth screening tools (WealthEngine, iWave, DonorSearch, and others) surface several types of indicators:

- **Real estate holdings:** Public record; often the most reliable single indicator of net worth
- **Stock and business ownership:** SEC filings for public company holdings; less visible for private ownership
- **Previous philanthropic gifts:** Gifts to other organizations that file public disclosures (primarily gifts to foundations and political campaigns)
- **Board service:** Signals civic engagement and philanthropic orientation

These tools typically produce a composite wealth rating (e.g., 1-10 scale or tiered buckets) and estimates of capacity at various gift levels.

### Limitations

**Capacity does not equal willingness.** This is the most important limitation to internalize. A high wealth rating means someone could give — it says nothing about whether they will. Common failure modes:

- High wealth with strong existing philanthropic commitments elsewhere (major donors to other institutions who have little capacity or inclination left)
- High wealth with no nonprofit giving history (philanthropically disengaged)
- Wealth derived from illiquid assets (real estate, private business) that doesn't translate to available charitable dollars

**False positives are common.** Treat a high wealth score as a reason to research further, not as a solicitation trigger.

**Coverage is incomplete.** Tools like WealthEngine typically return no usable data for 30-50% of records, particularly for younger donors, people with common names, and people whose wealth is held in private vehicles.

### Ethical Considerations

Use wealth data as one input among many. Avoid reducing a person to their rating. Don't allow wealth screening to replace relationship-building. Treat all prospect data with appropriate privacy care — donor information should not be shared outside the development function without a clear need.

## Email and Digital Engagement

### Typical Nonprofit Email Benchmarks

| Metric | Typical range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Open rate | 15-25% | Varies by list quality, subject line, audience segment |
| Click rate | 2-5% | Clicks indicate active interest, not just passive awareness |
| Unsubscribe rate | 0.2-0.5% | Above 1% signals content or frequency problems |

Note: Apple Mail Privacy Protection has inflated reported open rates since 2021. Use click rates and downstream behavior as more reliable engagement signals.

### Interpreting Engagement Signals

- **Opens** indicate awareness and at least passing interest. A contact who opens consistently is paying attention.
- **Clicks** indicate active interest — they wanted to know more. This is a stronger signal.
- **Sustained engagement over multiple campaigns** indicates affinity with your mission and content. This is the most meaningful signal.

A contact who has never opened a message in 24 months is effectively unreachable by email. Segment them out of active campaigns; don't waste sends or degrade your sender reputation.

### Engagement and Giving

Research on email engagement and giving consistently shows that engaged subscribers are meaningfully more likely to donate than disengaged ones. A rough practitioner's estimate: engaged subscribers (regular openers and clickers) are **3-5x more likely** to donate than disengaged ones on the same list.

Practical implication: when identifying prospects who have never given, email engagement data is one of the best available signals of readiness. A subscriber who has opened your last six newsletters is a warmer prospect than one who hasn't engaged in two years, regardless of their wealth score.

### Segmentation for Email

Do not send the same message to every segment. At minimum, differentiate:

- **Prospects (never donated):** Mission-first messaging; introduce IASC's impact; no hard ask or a very soft one
- **Lapsed donors:** Acknowledge the prior relationship; share what's changed; graduated ask
- **Active donors:** Stewardship and impact reporting; upgrade ask only when relationship supports it

## Metrics That Matter

| Metric | Definition | Benchmark |
|---|---|---|
| Donor retention rate | % of donors who give again the next year | 40-45% average; 60%+ is strong |
| Donor lifetime value (LTV) | Total expected giving over the relationship | Drives cultivation investment decisions |
| Average first gift | What new donors typically give | $50-150 for small nonprofits |
| Average gift growth | How giving increases over time for retained donors | Major donors often give 10-100x their first gift over time |
| Cost per dollar raised | Total development cost / total revenue raised | Major gifts: $0.05-0.20; direct mail: $0.20-0.50; events: $0.50+ |
| Re-activation rate | % of lapsed donors who give again | 5-15% typical |

Use these benchmarks to set realistic expectations and evaluate program health, not as rigid targets. Small organizations with specialized audiences (like academic publishers) often have different patterns than sector averages.
